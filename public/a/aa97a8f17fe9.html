HOW IT WORKS

The Anatomy of a Home Ed WhatsApp Community

This is a WhatsApp Community with a central noticeboard and 14 sub-chats, each for a specific activity or venue. One person, Rachel Allen, created and administrates almost all of them. The structure reveals a clear hierarchy.

📢

Central Noticeboard

3,000+ messages. The main "What's on" chat. Links shared, events discussed, subchats created. Acts as discovery layer and coordination hub simultaneously.

📌

Activity Sub-chats

14 dedicated groups for specific activities: Field Trip Fridays, Nature Club, Lego Robotics, First Aid, museum visits, seasonal events. Each one a mini-project.

👩‍💻

One Super Admin

Rachel created 14 of 15 groups. She researches venues, negotiates pricing, manages bookings, chases payments, creates polls, and coordinates across chats.

KEY FINDING

The Super Admin Problem

The entire community rests on Rachel's shoulders. She is simultaneously event researcher, venue negotiator, booking manager, payment collector, communications officer, and group moderator. The chats reveal this is unsustainable.

"I've been overwhelmed with Little Street admin... which is still ongoing with emails and phone calls etc back and forth so put the first aid admin on hold." — Rachel Allen, First Aid Session chat
Evidence from the chats: Rachel personally researches venues, emails providers, negotiates group rates, creates WhatsApp polls for dates, manually tallies attendee numbers across messages, sends individual payment requests via private message, tracks who has paid against bank transfers (often under different names), sends reminders, manages waitlists, handles cancellations, and coordinates late arrivals. For a single Godstone Farm trip, this involved 50+ admin messages over multiple days.

PAIN POINT #1

Payment Collection is a Nightmare

Every group booking follows the same painful pattern. This is exactly the workflow Fieldwise replaces.

1️⃣

Manual Price Calculation

Rachel calculates cost per head based on total attendees. "For 30 people, probably minimum of £7.30 per person." Price changes as numbers shift, requiring recalculation and re-communication.

2️⃣

Bank Transfer Collection

Payment details sent by private message. Parents pay via bank transfer. Some pay under different names. No reference numbers. Rachel manually cross-references payments against a spreadsheet or handwritten list.

3️⃣

Chasing and Reconciliation

"Ask for a reference as I didn't... then you have people who pay under different names and it takes a little while to figure it out." Payments due dates missed. Manual chase-up required.

4️⃣

Financial Risk on the Admin

Rachel pays the venue upfront, then collects from families. If someone drops out or doesn't pay, she absorbs the loss. Zero protection, zero automation.

PAIN POINT #2

Headcount Management is Chaos

Every event requires manually counting who is coming, how many children, how many adults, who is participating vs supervising, and whether minimums are met. This plays out publicly in the chat.

  • FIRST AID Rachel sends 10+ individual messages tagging people: "I have you down as 2 people. Please can you confirm." "I have you down as 5 people for session 2." Each response requires manual tally updates.
  • BUTCHERY "So far there are 10 people in this chat. Maybe do a poll when more have joined? Like 15+?" — gauging demand through chat membership, not actual bookings.
  • GODSTONE Complex adult-to-child ratios for free entry: "people coming with 1 child who got 2 free adult tickets as they basically used up the free adult ratio." Rachel manually redistributes spare ratios across families.
  • THEATRE "I haven't done admin in a long time. I need to brush up on my skills." A second parent (Tash) volunteers to organise the panto and immediately discovers how hard it is.
  • SESSION SPLIT First Aid had to split into two sessions. Rachel manually assigns families to Session 1 or Session 2, then tracks capacity: "Session 1 will be full now."

PAIN POINT #3

Information Gets Buried and Lost

Critical details vanish in the message stream. Parents join late, miss context, and ask questions that have already been answered. The admin re-explains repeatedly.

"Gosh 100 messages 😂 what day and time is this sorted for please?" — Zoe, First Aid Session chat (joining a fast-moving thread)
  • PATTERN Rachel uses the group description as a pseudo-database, updating it repeatedly (sometimes 4-5 edits in a row) to store current event details. But most people don't read descriptions.
  • PATTERN Newcomers ask "how do we book?" or "what's the price?" long after it was discussed. Rachel forwards old messages and re-explains.
  • PATTERN Facebook links are the primary discovery mechanism: events found on Facebook are re-shared into WhatsApp with copied text. The information chain is Facebook post → WhatsApp message → group description edit → verbal reminder.
  • PATTERN "I posted about this on the Facebook group but I don't think anyone else wants to go?" — cross-platform fragmentation means the same event gets different visibility on different channels.
Fieldwise solves this: A persistent listing page with date, time, location, price, capacity, and booking status replaces the entire message chain. One URL instead of 100 messages.

PAIN POINT #4

Venue Negotiation is Exhausting

Securing group rates, understanding pricing structures, and negotiating with venues is a recurring burden. The chats reveal this takes days of back-and-forth emails.

  • PRICING Daisy First Aid changed their pricing between the initial enquiry and booking. "It was originally £90 between up to 30 children. But as it is adults and children, she has changed it." Rachel had to re-negotiate and re-communicate to the group.
  • COMPLEXITY Godstone Farm pricing: group booking rate, SEND group rate (for free adult entry), child-to-adult ratios, optional activities, animal feed costs — all manually calculated and explained across multiple messages.
  • PROOF The Hawth theatre demanded "proof of home education" for discounted tickets. Tash spent an entire day emailing back and forth: "I cannot tell you how long it took to explain this and for them to understand."
  • GHOSTING "I didn't get a reply for a month but then sent another message and she replied." Venues not responding to individual parent enquiries.
  • VENUE ACCESS Rachel has spent 4 years trying to access free community spaces: "churches... always a no", "children's centres... a firm NO", "I've discussed with many churches." The venue barrier is real and persistent.

WHO RUNS THIS

The Super Admin (Rachel)
Community Builder

Creates groups, researches venues, negotiates rates, manages bookings and payments, provides emotional support, connects newcomers. Spends 10+ hours/week on unpaid admin. Has been doing this for 4+ years. Frequently overwhelmed but deeply committed.

PRICING DYNAMICS

How the Community Thinks About Money

The chats reveal nuanced pricing behaviour that directly informs Fieldwise's fee model.

  • SENSITIVITY Rachel always emphasises low cost: "I always try to keep cost as low as possible." Donations basis used for Nature Club: "If £3 per child is out of reach for you then please do still come along and give what you can from £0+."
  • WILLINGNESS But parents do pay for quality: £10-13 for first aid, £10.60 for Godstone, £22/head for the panto, £30/child for deer butchery. The threshold for perceived value is around £10-15 per session.
  • ADMIN FEE Tash asked for "an extra £1 for my time organising" the panto. This was the first mention of compensating admin work — and she felt she needed to justify it. There is no established norm for paying organisers.
  • COST SHARING Total cost divided by headcount is the standard model. Price per person changes as numbers shift. "For 30 people, probably minimum of £7.30 per person." This uncertainty is uncomfortable for families budgeting tightly.
  • VENUE COSTS "Many places charge £20-35+ an hour which is a lot." Venue hire is the hidden cost that either gets passed to families or absorbed by the organiser.
Fieldwise implication: The 10% platform fee model is sound. Parents already accept small add-ons (£1 admin, variable headcount pricing). A transparent "payment processing fee" of 40-50p on a £15 session is within tolerance. The key is that the listed price doesn't visibly inflate.

The minimum number problem is real

Almost every group booking in these chats has a minimum attendee requirement. The entire admin burden exists to hit that number.

  • FIRST AID "We will need minimum numbers to ensure the class goes ahead... at least 9 adult + child tickets." Rachel manually counted across 20+ messages to confirm viability.
  • GODSTONE Required minimum group size for educational visit pricing. Rachel gathered 15 children across the chat before confirming the booking.
  • THEATRE The Hawth requires 10 people for group booking discount. "I've personally never met that 10 person minimum so we have never been able to do a group booking." Until the group grew large enough.
  • BUTCHERY Provider can accommodate 10 children at £30 each. "If at least a few people message to say hey we are interested, he will be more inclined to offer something."
  • LEGO "6 is the minimum required to go ahead. We will wait until Monday and then make a decision about going ahead based on numbers."
This is Fieldwise's minimum threshold feature in the wild. Every single one of these scenarios would be automated: families book, the platform aggregates demand, and the session confirms when the minimum is met. No manual counting, no chasing, no risk to the organiser.

NEW INSIGHTS

What the Chats Reveal That Isn't in the Business Plan

Admin Compensation

Tash asking for £1/ticket for her time is a signal. There's no mechanism to compensate community organisers. Fieldwise could enable a transparent "organiser fee" that legitimises this labour. This could be a powerful provider-side hook.

Proof of Home Education

Multiple discussions about venues demanding "proof" that doesn't formally exist. Fieldwise could become a de facto verification layer — not LA-connected, but a trusted community membership that venues accept.

Sub-chat Proliferation Fatigue

15 groups for one community. Parents join multiple chats and lose track. "I'm confused reading these messages now!" Messages get posted in the wrong chat. The structure that enables organisation also creates fragmentation.

Cross-community Coordination

Multiple references to "the East Grinstead home edders", other WhatsApp communities, and Facebook groups. Events are cross-posted manually. There's no way for communities to share events without the admin doing it.

THE STRATEGIC INSIGHT

Every community has a Rachel

The single most important takeaway from this analysis is structural. Every home ed community across the UK has someone like Rachel — a parent-volunteer who holds the entire local ecosystem together through sheer force of will and WhatsApp admin.

These people are:

  • BURNING OUT "I've been overwhelmed with Little Street admin." The workload scales linearly with community size. More families = more admin = more burnout.
  • SINGLE POINT OF FAILURE If Rachel stops, 15 groups and 50+ families lose their coordination layer overnight. There is no backup, no handover process, no institutional knowledge.
  • NOT COMPENSATED Zero pay for 10+ hours/week of skilled work: negotiation, communication, financial management, community building.
  • THE IDEAL EARLY ADOPTER Rachel and people like her are the exact person Fieldwise is built for. If the platform saves her 5 hours a week, she becomes a missionary for it.
Go-to-market insight: Don't sell Fieldwise to "the home ed community." Sell it to the Rachels. Find the super-admin in every local community. Solve their problem. They'll bring their entire network with them.
